(M)Ad Man

A look at the state of advertising by an ad man.

Summer 2015

“I’m a hypocrite. I don’t consume my own product. I don’t watch TV that has commercials... I hate pre-roll. I go to another screen, count to 15 and then come back. I hate it. I don’t want to look at it.” The way Collin Sheehan sees it, advertising has come a long way since the time of “booze and cigarettes” and everything else from the Mad-Men era. According to Collin, the one thing that will remain the same is telling visual stories. If you can tell an effective story—if you can make people feel something in a TV-ad—you’re already on your way to producing pre-roll, online video, and Vines. As advertising continues to evolve, so does the way students prepare for a career in it. Collin explained, “I think I’m the last guy to get a job without going to a portfolio center or something like that,” and there’s good and bad that comes with it. “I think there’s probably a lot of really great minds out there that might not go to a portfolio school,” yet he thinks that the advertisements that generally come from a portfolio school are good. At the same time, he pointed out, “there’s a pattern to [these ads]... it’s a race to simplicity and minimizing branding... and who knows what the next best thing is going to be?” At the same time, Collin says that “it definitely accelerates you about four or five years in the business... and it’s a great pattern.”

In the age of Ad-Blockers, the power is with the consumers to decide whether to consume it, and “typically you’re only going to consume it if there’s some kind of entertainment value to you... it’s on [the advertisers] to be more interesting.” As new technologies continue to emerge, more media becomes available. As a consumer, Collin embraces the change by switching the channel when he hears a TV spot and running to the bathroom during pre-roll on YouTube. Like most modern audiences, he scours the web for good content. “I’ll go on reddit and if someone has a funny enough ad, or if someone makes something funny, or if it’s forwarded to me — that’s how I consume it.”

Collin acknowledges that a lot has happened to the world during his last ten years in advertising. “I didn’t get an email [address] until I got out of college... but anyone born after that had an email in elementary school. Now kids have iPads.” During our conversation, began to express his fear that our world will become one where nobody ever looks up from a screen—but he stopped himself. “We’re already there. People don’t walk down the street the same way... even people who live in New York suck at walking in New York because their face is planted into a screen, where before they walked with purpose.” Collin agrees that advertising is driving that, because “advertising is what pays for a lot of that content that people are consuming... The blood is on our hands.”

Some media is bad, some media is good, and a very small portion of media is truly excellent. Advertising is no different. Filmmakers scoff at Hollywood flops, writers avoid predictable stories, and advertisers visit the bathroom when a second-rate commercials interrupts their TV show. People who create media do it to create something that moves people, and Collin is no different. “I grew up watching commercials that gave me goosebumps. I respond emotionally to stuff like that, whether it be a piece of music, or TV, or a movie... or you know, a commercial.”
